2. Formal Definition of Similarity
•
Inverse of a distance function (metric)
–
Sim(S1, S2)  [0, 1]
–
Sim(S1, S2) = 1  objects S1 and S2 are identical
–
Sim(S1, S2) = 0  S1 and S2 have has no common characteristics
–
Similarity increases with the commonality between S1 and S2, and decreases             
with their difference
–
Similarity and distance are inverse to each other
–
Sim(S1, S1) = 1     Reflexivity
–
Sim(S1, S2) = Sim(S2, S1)     Symmetricity
–
Sim(S1, S3) ≥ Sim(S1, S2) × Sim(S2, S3)     Triangular inequality 
•
Dist(S1, S3) ≤ Dist(S1, S2) + Dist(S2, S3)

3. Main Categories of Similarity Approaches
•
A range of algorithms for comparing semi-structured data have been proposed
– Most of them exploit CPM and AI techniques
• Rigorously structured data
• Fine-grained
• Main target: data structure
– Others focus on extending conventional IR methods, e.g. the vector space model, 
so as to provide semi-structured similarity assessment
• Loosely structured data
• Coarse-grained
• Main target: data content
– Other diverse methods
• Edge/Path Similarity, Fast Fourier Transform, Entropy…
• Usually approximate Tree Edit Distance

5. Information Retrieval based Approaches
•
Traditional IR methods are not adequate with semi-structured data!
– With semi-structured data, content is distributed at different levels of the 
document tree
• It is to be treated differently w.r.t. flat content to improve retrieval precision
– Down-weighting TF-IDF statistics w.r.t. structure
– Users in turn want to refer to document structure when searching for relevant 
information
• Formulating Content-and-structure queries, in comparison with the content-only queries 
in conventional IR
• Specifying search context (query scope)

•
To answer the question, we need to cover some basic concepts:
– Data: the lowest abstraction of data representation with no meaning 
• Example: “2001” is considered as a number consisting of 4 digits
– Information: The interpretation of the data by giving it well-defined meaning
• Example “2001” is  “the year of announcement of the Semantic Web"
– Meta-data:  A description about the data and/or information 
• Such as who gave the data/information (e.g., Wikipedia), when was the data/ 
information given (e.g., published in 2002, etc.)
