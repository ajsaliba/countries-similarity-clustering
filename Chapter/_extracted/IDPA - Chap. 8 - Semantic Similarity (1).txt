Gloss-based Approach
Problem: The original gloss-based measure compares glosses by 
counting the number of words that are shared between them
It does not differentiate between single word and phrasal overlaps
Treating each gloss as a bag of words 
Improvement: based on Zipfian [1] relationship between phrases
The longer the phrase, the less likely it is to occur multiple times in a given corpus
An n-word overlap is much rarer than a single word overlap
– Assigning an n-word overlap the score of n2
• Giving it a score higher than the sum of the scores assigned to 
those n words when they occur separately

Gloss-based Approach
Improved method:
SimGloss+(c1, c2)   = |gloss(c1) LCS gloss (c2)|
Overlap operator defined based on Longest Common Sequence
Of one or more consecutive words that occurs in both glosses 
Overall process: given two strings:
1. The LCS between them is detected and removed
– A unique marker is placed in its place in each of the two input strings
2. The two strings thus obtained are then again checked for overlaps 
– The process is repeated until there are no longer any overlaps
3. The sizes of the overlaps thus found are squared and added together to 
    compute the similarity score

Data (information) is usually coded in semi-structured syntax
Knowledge is usually represented in SW standards

Coding  syntax (tagging, syntactic properties, etc.)
Representation model  formalism (tree-based, graph-based, network-based, etc.)
Semi-structured tree-based model is good for data (information), yet it is not

  Hence the introduction of RDF and OWL graph-based models

Conclusion
Edge-based semantic similarity
Pro: Intuitive
Con: Depends on varying link distances
Node-based semantic similarity
Pro: Gets round varying link distances 
Con: Requires corpus-based statistics
Gloss-based semantic similarity
Pro: Makes use of additional information in glosses
Con: Limited use of semantic relations between concepts
Empirical results usually show that node-based and integrated 
methods are generally better predictors of semantic similarity
